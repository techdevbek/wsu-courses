\item
For each part, justify you claim as to whether or not $S$ is a basis for the indicated vector space.
\begin{enumerate}
\item  Is $S=\{\left[\begin{array}{cc}
1&0\\-2&1\end{array}\right],\ \left[\begin{array}{cc}
0&-1\\1&1\end{array}\right], \ \left[ \begin{array}{cc}
-1&2\\1&0\end{array}\right]
, \ \left[ \begin{array}{cc}
2&1\\-4&4\end{array}\right]\}$ a basis for $M_{2\times 2}(\mathbb{R})$?
\item is $S=\{\left[\begin{array}{cc}
1&0\\-2&1\end{array}\right],\ \left[\begin{array}{cc}
0&-1\\1&1\end{array}\right], \ \left[ \begin{array}{cc}
-1&2\\1&0\end{array}\right]
, \ \left[ \begin{array}{cc}
2&1\\2&-2\end{array}\right]\}$  a basis for $M_{2\times 2}(\mathbb{R})$?
\item Is $S=\{x^3+2x^2, -x^2+3x+1,x^3-x^2+2x-1, 1+x\}$ a basis for $\mathbb{P}_3$?
\item Is $S=\{x^3-x, 2x^2+4,-2x^3+3x^2+2x+6,x+2,x^2+2x+3\}$  a basis for $\mathbb{P}_3$?
\end{enumerate}

\item Let $u$, $v$, and $w$ be distinct vectors of a vector space $V$. Show that if $\{u, v, w\}$ is a basis for $V$, then $\{u, u+v,u+v+w\}$ is also a basis for $V$.

\item Prove that a vector space is infinite-dimensional if and only if it contains an infinite linearly independent subset.
    
 \item Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{C}$ with dimension $n$. Prove that if $V$ is now regarded as a vector space over $\mathbb{R}$, then dim $V=2n$.


     \end{enumerate}
